## From p-adic numbers to cryptographic attacks

In the [previous article](/blog/p-adic-numbers), we built the $p$-adic numbers
from scratch: the $p$-adic absolute value, the ultrametric world it creates, the
construction of $\mathbb{Z}_p$ as infinite digit expansions, and Hensel lifting
as the $p$-adic analogue of Newton's method. All of that was pure mathematics.

Now we put it to work. The $p$-adic numbers are not just number-theoretic
decoration. They appear in a concrete, devastating attack on a specific class of
elliptic curves used in cryptography.

## Smart's attack on anomalous elliptic curves

Let $E$ be an elliptic curve over $\mathbb{F}_p$, with a generator $P$. In
elliptic curve cryptography, the secret key is an integer $n$ and the public key
is the point $Q = nP$. The elliptic curve discrete logarithm problem (ECDLP)
asks: given $P$ and $Q$, recover $n$. Normally this is hard. That hardness is
the foundation of ECDSA, ECDH, and most elliptic curve cryptography.

But there is a degenerate case. If $\#E(\mathbb{F}_p) = p$, the curve is called
**anomalous**.
[Nigel Smart](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigel_Smart_(cryptographer)>)
showed in 1999 that the ECDLP on anomalous curves can be solved in linear time.
The mechanism goes through $p$-adic numbers.

Here is the idea. Three groups are in play, and the notation can be confusing,
so let us spell them out:

- $E(\mathbb{F}_p)$: the group of points on the original curve over the finite
  field. This is where cryptography happens. It is a finite group of $p$ points
  (since the curve is anomalous).
- $\tilde{E}(\mathbb{Q}_p)$: the group of points on a **lifted** curve over
  $\mathbb{Q}_p$. We choose a curve $\tilde{E}$ with $p$-adic coefficients that
  reduces to $E$ modulo $p$. This is an infinite group. Every point in
  $E(\mathbb{F}_p)$ lifts to a point in $\tilde{E}(\mathbb{Q}_p)$.
- $\hat{E}(p\mathbb{Z}_p)$: the **formal group**, consisting of points on
  $\tilde{E}$ that reduce to the identity (the point at infinity) modulo $p$.
  Here $p\mathbb{Z}_p = \{x \in \mathbb{Z}_p : |x|_p \leq 1/p\}$ is the ideal of
  $p$-adic integers divisible by $p$, i.e. those whose first digit $a_0$ is
  zero. The formal group is the kernel of the reduction map
  $\tilde{E}(\mathbb{Q}_p) \to E(\mathbb{F}_p)$. It captures the "purely
  $p$-adic" part of the lifted curve.

### Step-by-step walkthrough

Let us walk through the attack concretely enough that you could reimplement it.

**Step 1: Lift the curve.** Take the equation of $E$ (say $y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$
over $\mathbb{F}_p$) and choose a curve $\tilde{E}$ over $\mathbb{Q}_p$ that
reduces to $E$ mod $p$. The simplest way: keep the same coefficients $a, b$ but
view them as $p$-adic integers, then perturb them by adding random multiples of
$p$. For instance $\tilde{a} = a + r \cdot p$ for random $r$. The perturbation
ensures that the lifted curve is not the "canonical" lift (where the next step
would give a trivial zero).

**Step 2: Lift the points.** For a point $P = (x_0, y_0) \in E(\mathbb{F}_p)$,
find a point $\tilde{P} \in \tilde{E}(\mathbb{Q}_p)$ that reduces to $P$ mod
$p$. Concretely: plug $x_0$ (viewed as a $p$-adic integer) into the curve
equation $\tilde{y}^2 = x_0^3 + \tilde{a} x_0 + \tilde{b}$, take the square root
in $\mathbb{Q}_p$ (which exists by
[Hensel lifting](/blog/p-adic-numbers#hensel-lifting), since $y_0 \neq 0$), and
pick the root that reduces to $y_0$ mod $p$. Do the same for $Q$.

**Step 3: Multiply by $p$.** Compute $p \cdot \tilde{P}$ and $p \cdot \tilde{Q}$
using the elliptic curve group law over $\mathbb{Q}_p$. Why does this help? In
$E(\mathbb{F}_p)$, the point $P$ has order $p$ (since $\#E(\mathbb{F}_p) = p$),
so $p \cdot P = \mathcal{O}$ (the identity). When we reduce $p \cdot \tilde{P}$
mod $p$, we get $\mathcal{O}$. That means the coordinates of $p \cdot \tilde{P}$
are divisible by $p$: this point lies in the formal group
$\hat{E}(p\mathbb{Z}_p)$.

**Step 4: Extract the logarithm.** A point in the formal group has coordinates
$(x, y)$ where $v_p(x) \leq -2$ and $v_p(y) \leq -3$ (the coordinates blow up
$p$-adically). The **local parameter** is $t = -x/y$, which satisfies
$v_p(t) \geq 1$, so $t \in p\mathbb{Z}_p$. The formal group logarithm is a power
series

$$\log_{\hat{E}}(t) = t + c_2 t^2 + c_3 t^3 + \cdots$$

that converges for $t \in p\mathbb{Z}_p$. The key property: this logarithm is a
**group homomorphism** from $\hat{E}(p\mathbb{Z}_p)$ (with the elliptic curve
group law) to $p\mathbb{Z}_p$ (with plain addition). To first order,
$\log_{\hat{E}}(t) \approx t$, and for the attack we only need the leading term.

So define:

$$\phi(\tilde{P}) = -\frac{x(p\tilde{P})}{y(p\tilde{P})} \in p\mathbb{Z}_p$$

This $\phi$ maps the elliptic curve group law to addition.

**Step 5: Divide.** Since $Q = nP$ on the original curve, the lifts satisfy
$p\tilde{Q} = n \cdot (p\tilde{P})$ in the formal group (up to an error that
vanishes to high $p$-adic order). Applying $\phi$:

$$\phi(\tilde{Q}) = n \cdot \phi(\tilde{P}) \quad \text{in } p\mathbb{Z}_p$$

Now $n$ is just a ratio of two $p$-adic numbers:

$$n = \frac{\phi(\tilde{Q})}{\phi(\tilde{P})} \mod p$$

Division in $\mathbb{Q}_p$ is easy. Reduce mod $p$ to get the secret key as an
integer in $\{0, \ldots, p-1\}$.

### Why the anomalous condition is essential

The whole argument hinges on step 3: $p \cdot \tilde{P}$ must land in the formal
group but must not be zero. The anomalous condition $\#E(\mathbb{F}_p) = p$
guarantees that $P$ has order exactly $p$ in $E(\mathbb{F}_p)$, so $p\tilde{P}$
reduces to the identity. If the group order were different (say $p + 1$ or some
other value), then $p \cdot P \neq \mathcal{O}$ in $E(\mathbb{F}_p)$, and
$p\tilde{P}$ would not land in the formal group at all. The logarithm would have
nothing to work with.

The takeaway for implementers: never use an anomalous curve. Any reasonable
curve generation procedure checks $\#E(\mathbb{F}_p) \neq p$, but the attack
illustrates something deeper. The $p$-adic structure was always there,
underlying the curve arithmetic. The anomalous condition simply makes it visible
and exploitable.

## The broader picture

Beyond Smart's attack, $p$-adic analysis appears throughout modern number theory
in ways that feed back into cryptography. $p$-adic $L$-functions encode deep
arithmetic information about elliptic curves and modular forms. The Iwasawa main
conjecture (now a theorem in many cases) relates $p$-adic $L$-functions to the
structure of certain Galois modules, connecting analytic and algebraic
invariants. This circle of ideas informs our understanding of why certain
number-theoretic problems are hard and others are not, which is ultimately what
cryptographic security rests on.
