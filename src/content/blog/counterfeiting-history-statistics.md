## TL;DR

- **Counterfeiting is as old as money itself** (the first Greek fakes date to
  the 6th century BC) and has repeatedly served as a weapon of economic warfare
  — the British against the _Continentals_ and the assignats, Nazi Germany
  against the pound sterling (Operation Bernhard), the USSR and North Korea
  against the dollar.
- **Today, the counterfeiting of major currencies is historically low in
  proportion**: only **14 fake notes per million** genuine euros in 2025
  (444,000 withdrawn, ECB) and a stock of counterfeit dollars estimated at
  **$15–30 million (1 note in 40,000 to 80,000)** by the Federal Reserve — a far
  cry from the era of American _free banking_ when, at the founding of the
  Secret Service (1865), a third to a half of the currency was fake.
- **The loss falls on the last good-faith holder** (merchant or individual),
  with no reimbursement; the macroeconomic impact is small, but counterfeiting
  remains a vector for financing organized crime and a threat to confidence —
  its invisible foundation.

## Introduction: Confidence, the invisible foundation of money

Money is worth nothing in itself. A 50-euro note is just a printed piece of
cotton; a gold coin feeds no one until it is exchanged. What gives money its
value is something impalpable but essential: **confidence**. Confidence that
others will accept this note tomorrow just as they do today; confidence in the
authority — sovereign, central bank, state — that guarantees its integrity. The
very term "fiduciary money" comes from the Latin _fiducia_, confidence.

Counterfeiting is precisely the most direct attack on this foundation. By
introducing units without real backing into circulation, the forger does not
only steal from the person who receives the fake: he dilutes the value of the
entire money supply, fuels inflation and, above all, corrodes collective
confidence. This is why, since antiquity, counterfeiting has been treated not as
a simple theft but as a crime against sovereign authority — often punishable by
death. It is also why states have seen counterfeiting as a weapon of war: to
destroy the enemy's money is to destroy its capacity to finance and govern
itself.

This study is divided into two parts. The first traces the history of
counterfeiting, from archaic Greece to the digital age. The second draws up a
worldwide statistical picture of the phenomenon, drawing on the most recent
official data (ECB, Federal Reserve, US Secret Service, INTERPOL, Europol)
through 2025–2026.

# PART 1: A HISTORY OF COUNTERFEITING (Thematic Chronology)

## Chapter 1 — Antiquity and the Middle Ages: fraud on precious metal

### Ancient Greece: the first fakes in Western history

Counterfeiting is almost as old as money itself, which appeared in Lydia (Asia
Minor) around 600 BC. As early as the 6th century BC, we find **plated coins**
(_fourrée_, from the French "fourré/garni"; in Latin _subaeratus_, "copper
underneath"; in Greek _hypochalkos_): a core of base metal (copper, bronze)
covered with a thin layer of silver or gold.

A famous archaeological example comes from the sanctuary of Poseidon near
Corinth: among a hoard of Aeginetan "turtle" staters (6th c. BC), the American
excavators found a copper coin plated with silver, as well as several coins
consisting of a simple silver shell filled with clay or sand — counted among the
first known fakes of the Western world.
[Snible](https://www.snible.org/coins/fakes/dodson.html) Athens also knew the
problem: the lawgiver **Solon** (early 6th c. BC) instituted a law punishing the
fraudulent reproduction of the city's coinage with death.
[Snible](https://www.snible.org/coins/fakes/dodson.html) The Athenians employed
a verifying official (_dokimastes_) tasked with testing suspicious coins; many
surviving Athenian "owls" bear _test cuts_. Notably, during the Peloponnesian
War, Athens itself issued in 406 BC an emergency plated coinage (copper core,
silver plating),
[Armstrong Economics](https://www.armstrongeconomics.com/fourree-counterfeiting-in-ancient-times/)
proof that even the state could resort to monetary debasement in times of
crisis.

### Rome: official debasement and private fakes

Rome offers the most documented example of **official devaluation**
(_debasement_) — not criminal counterfeiting, but state fraud on precious-metal
content. The silver **denarius** (_denarius_), introduced around 211 BC,
originally contained about 4.5 g of nearly pure silver
[LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/roman-currency-debasement-brief-history-von-wooding)
(a fineness close to 95–98% under Augustus).
[TheCollector](https://www.thecollector.com/inflation-third-century-crisis/)

The chronology of its debasement is telling:

- **AD 64**: the emperor **Nero** reduced the weight of the denarius (from
  1/72nd to 1/96th of a Roman pound) and lowered its silver fineness from about
  99% to ~94%. The silver thus recovered financed the rebuilding of Rome after
  the Great Fire.
  [JM Bullion](https://www.jmbullion.com/investing-guide/numismatics/the-roman-denarius-coin/)
- **2nd century**: under Trajan, the fineness fell to about 85%.
  [Hard Money History](https://www.hardmoneyhistory.com/denarius-silver-content/)
- **AD 215**: Caracalla introduced the _antoninianus_, nominally worth two
  denarii but containing only about 1.5 times the silver — accelerating
  inflation.
  [JM Bullion](https://www.jmbullion.com/investing-guide/numismatics/the-roman-denarius-coin/)
- **Under Septimius Severus**: the content dropped to ~50%.
  [Hard Money History](https://www.hardmoneyhistory.com/denarius-silver-content/)
- **Around AD 250–268** (reign of Gallienus): the denarius contained no more
  than ~5% silver or less, becoming a bronze token with a thin silver wash.
  [Hard Money History](https://www.hardmoneyhistory.com/denarius-silver-content/)

To mask this debasement, the Roman mints employed **surface-enrichment**
techniques giving the illusion of higher content.
[Hard Money History](https://www.hardmoneyhistory.com/denarius-silver-content/)
The consequences were catastrophic: hyperinflation, soldiers refusing to be paid
in coin, a partial return to barter
[Hard Money History](https://www.hardmoneyhistory.com/denarius-silver-content/)
— one of the drivers of the "Crisis of the Third Century." Against private
forgers, Rome legislated: the _lex Cornelia de falsis_ of **Sulla** reintroduced
serrated edges (_serrati_) on silver coins; the praetor **Marius Gratidianus**
(in the 80s BC) was celebrated for devising methods to detect false coins.
[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourr%C3%A9e)

### The Middle Ages: clipping and repression

In the Middle Ages, the dominant fraud was **clipping**: shaving small amounts
of metal from the edges of coins — rarely perfectly round — then melting down
and reselling the shavings.
[The National Archives](https://www.nationalarchives.gov.uk/education/resources/jews-in-england-1290/)
To this were added counterfeiting by alloy and clandestine workshops.

Medieval authorities treated these crimes as attacks on the sovereign majesty
(the king's effigy being debased), punishable by mutilation or death. The most
dramatic episode occurred in **England under Edward I**. In 1278, the value of
the coinage in circulation having fallen by half,
[The Jewish Chronicle](https://www.thejc.com/news/the-medieval-monarch-who-made-a-mint-from-jewish-persecution-okvg2bbh)
the Crown ordered the mass arrest, on **17 November 1278**, of more than 600
Jews (according to some sources up to 680 held in the Tower of London) as well
as Christians, accused of clipping.
[Haaretz](https://www.haaretz.com/jewish/2013-11-17/ty-article/.premium/1278-all-english-jews-arrested-by-crown/0000017f-db8e-db5a-a57f-dbee03bf0002)
Following the trials, **269 Jews were hanged in London** (and 29 Christians),
[The Jewish Chronicle](https://www.thejc.com/news/the-medieval-monarch-who-made-a-mint-from-jewish-persecution-okvg2bbh)
more than 10% of the kingdom's Jewish population.
[The Jewish Chronicle](https://www.thejc.com/news/the-medieval-monarch-who-made-a-mint-from-jewish-persecution-okvg2bbh)
Beyond the defense of the coinage, the campaign served fiscal aims
(confiscations)
[Medievalists](https://www.medievalists.net/2025/11/medieval-england-coin-clipping-scandal/)
and foreshadowed the **Edict of Expulsion of the Jews of 1290**. The English
theorist **Henry de Bracton** held that the worst punishments should be reserved
for counterfeiters and clippers. [CoinWeek](https://coinweek.com/clipped-coins/)
In Venice, clipping was punished by the removal of the eyes and the right hand,
by banishment and a heavy fine. [CoinWeek](https://coinweek.com/clipped-coins/)
England also established institutional controls such as the _Trial of the Pyx_,
the periodic verification of the fineness and weight of minted coins.

## Chapter 2 — The age of paper money (17th–19th century)

The rise of paper money, with the first banks of issue, opened an entirely new
field: it was infinitely easier to reproduce a print than to counterfeit a metal
alloy. Counterfeiting then also became a **weapon of economic warfare**.

### The assignats of the French Revolution and British counterfeiting

The **assignat**, fiduciary money issued under the French Revolution (a
debt-bond of 1789 secured on national property,
[Aprogemere](https://www.aprogemere.fr/vrai-ou-faux-assignats?iccaldate=2025-7-1)
turned into circulating money in 1791), is a textbook case. The massive issues
caused hyperinflation, but the phenomenon was aggravated by large-scale
counterfeiting. **Fake assignats were manufactured in Belgium, the Netherlands,
Germany, Switzerland and Great Britain**, the latter accused of acting with the
complicity of its government, an enemy of France interested in accelerating the
French economic crisis.
[Jimdofree](https://revolutionfrancaise1789.jimdofree.com/au-temps-de-la-r%C3%A9volution-fran%C3%A7aise/les-assignats/)
British workshops, such as the paper mill at Haughton Castle (Northumberland),
were identified in the 1790s as producing fake assignats.
[Assignat](https://assignat.fr/faux-assignats)

The threat was taken very seriously by the revolutionaries. As early as November
1790, the deputy Régnier declared that "the crime of the falsifier of assignats
is more dangerous than that of counterfeiters of coin," because it could destroy
confidence in the young Republic.
[OpenEdition](https://books.openedition.org/pumi/32048?lang=en) In 1793, the
General Directorate for the Manufacture of Assignats published a "collection of
records of distinctive signs" enabling fakes to be recognized.
[aprogemere](https://www.aprogemere.fr/vrai-ou-faux-assignats) The legislation
was severe: death penalty for makers and distributors; informing was rewarded
with bounties. [E-monsite](https://assignats.e-monsite.com/pages/page-15.html)
The fakes circulated all the more easily because a large part of the population
was illiterate. [E-monsite](https://assignats.e-monsite.com/pages/page-15.html)

### The American War of Independence: the "Continentals" counterfeited by the British

The clearest case of counterfeiting as a weapon of war in the 18th century is
that of the _Continental currency_, issued by the Continental Congress from 1775
[Ephemera Society of America](https://www.ephemerasociety.org/marbled-edge-continental-currency-challenged-british-counterfeiting/)
to finance the War of Independence. The British organized systematic
counterfeiting, notably from **occupied New York** and aboard the ship **HMS
_Phoenix_** anchored in New York harbor, which printed fake notes (especially
30-dollar denominations).
[Foundation for Economic Education](https://fee.org/articles/paper-money-as-a-weapon-of-war/)
Advertisements appeared openly in the loyalist New York newspapers: "Persons
going into other Colonies may be supplied with any Number of counterfeit
Congress Notes, for the Price of the Paper per Ream."
[Colonial Williamsburg](https://research.colonialwilliamsburg.org/Foundation/journal/Summer07/counterfeit.cfm)

**Benjamin Franklin** later testified that the fakes "emitted by the British
Government at New York were carried out and dispersed among the Inhabitants of
all the States, before the Fraud was detected," contributing to the depreciation
of the whole. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_American_currency)
Combined with over-issuance, this operation led to the collapse of the
_Continental_ ("not worth a continental") and to executions of forgers: **David
Farnsworth** and John Blair, caught with more than 10,000 dollars in fakes, were
tried and hanged in 1778 after George Washington took a personal interest in the
affair. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Farnsworth)

### The American pre-central-bank context: the "free banking" era

In the United States, after the demise of the Second Bank of the United States
(1836) and until the _National Banking Acts_ of 1863–1864, the country
experienced the **"free banking" era**. Thousands of state banks issued their
own notes, without any federal supervisory authority.
[ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/5025567_Wildcat_banking_banking_panics_and_free_banking_in_the_United_States)
This multiplicity — thousands of different note designs — made counterfeiting an
endemic scourge: no one could distinguish a genuine note from a well-executed
fake.
[Encyclopedia.com](https://www.encyclopedia.com/history/encyclopedias-almanacs-transcripts-and-maps/wildcat-banks)
The "wildcat banks," set up in remote places to discourage redemption in specie,
added to the confusion.
[Grokipedia](https://grokipedia.com/page/Wildcat_banking) A common fraud
consisted of erasing the name of a failed bank on worthless notes and replacing
it with that of a solvent bank.
[Gizmodo](https://gizmodo.com/an-illustrated-history-of-american-money-design-1743743361)

The scale of the phenomenon was considerable: according to the Federal Reserve
Bank of San Francisco, at the founding of the Secret Service (July 1865),
**"about one-third to one-half of the nation's currency was counterfeit."** It
was precisely to combat this scourge that the **United States Secret Service**
was created, on 14 April 1865, with the initial sole mission of suppressing
counterfeiting. During the Civil War, the Union also circulated fake Confederate
notes.

## Chapter 3 — The 20th century and the World Wars: counterfeiting as a weapon of state

### Operation Bernhard: the largest counterfeiting plot in history

**Operation Bernhard** is the most accomplished example of state counterfeiting.
Conceived by Nazi Germany as early as 1939 (under the initial name "Operation
Andreas"), it first aimed to **destabilize the British economy** by flooding the
United Kingdom with fake pounds sterling.
[Bank of England](https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/museum/online-collections/blog/operation-bernhard)

Relaunched in 1942 by Himmler under the direction of SS major **Bernhard
Krüger**, the operation changed its objective: to finance German intelligence
operations. Krüger recruited from the concentration camps prisoners (mostly
Jewish) skilled in engraving, printing, drawing
[British Brief](https://britbrief.co.uk/politics/diplomacy/nazi-banknote-forgery-truth-behind-peaky-blinders-film.html)
and banking. A team of **142 prisoners** was installed
[Bank of England](https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/museum/online-collections/blog/operation-bernhard)
in blocks 18 and 19 of the **Sachsenhausen** camp, near Berlin. The technical
challenge was considerable: reproducing the rag paper, the watermarks, and
cracking the algorithm of the serial numbers.
[History of Sorts](https://dirkdeklein.net/2016/05/14/operation-bernhard/)

The results were astonishing. At the time of the evacuation of Sachsenhausen
(April 1945, the stop order having been given by the RSHA on 22 February 1945),
the press there had produced **8,965,080 notes with a face value of
£134,610,810**
[Everything Everywhere](https://everything-everywhere.com/operation-bernhard/)
(total estimates range from £132.6 to £300 million).
[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Bernhard) The notes were
sorted by quality and disposed of through a laundering network run by SS major
**Friedrich Schwend** from Italy.
[Everything Everywhere](https://everything-everywhere.com/operation-bernhard/)
Fakes were used to pay the Turkish agent **Elyesa Bazna ("Cicero")**, and
£100,000 contributed to financing the operation to free Mussolini at Gran Sasso
(1943). [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Bernhard) The first
fake was detected in 1943 (a note that had passed through a British bank in
Morocco, whose serial number was already listed as "paid").
[Bank of England](https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/museum/online-collections/blog/operation-bernhard)
The Bank of England responded by withdrawing from circulation all denominations
above £5.
[Bank of England](https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/museum/online-collections/blog/operation-bernhard)
From 1944, the unit attempted to counterfeit the US 100-dollar bill, with the
help of the professional forger **Salomon Smolianoff**
[Everything Everywhere](https://everything-everywhere.com/operation-bernhard/);
the work was nearly completed but was never mass-produced, as the paper and the
serial numbers still posed problems.
[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Bernhard) At the end of the
war, much of the material and the fakes were thrown into Lake Toplitz, in
Austria.
[Bank of England](https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/museum/online-collections/blog/operation-bernhard)

### The Soviet dollar-counterfeiting operations (1920s–1930s)

Less well known, the Soviet dollar-counterfeiting operations targeted the
Federal Reserve's 100-dollar bills. According to the work of historian **Arnold
Krammer** (Texas A&M), Stalin ordered his services in the late 1920s to
counterfeit 100-dollar bills.
[Federal Reserve Bank of Richmond](https://www.richmondfed.org/-/media/richmondfedorg/publications/research/econ_focus/2012/q1/pdf/economic_history.pdf)
A trial held in Berlin (reported by _Time_ in 1933) revealed that in **1928 the
Soviet Politburo** had decided to produce fake pounds sterling and fake dollars
and Mexican pesos, in preparation for possible conflicts.
[Time](https://time.com/archive/6744166/russia-counterfeiting-explained/) The
affair involved a Soviet espionage network in the United States (the physician
Valentine Burtan and the agent Nicholas Dozenberg),
[Cambridge Core](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/slavic-review/article/abs/russian-counterfeit-dollars-a-case-of-early-soviet-espionage/0C2B21DF8635DA2403136AFEC364C775)
giving rise to federal prosecutions.

### Other state operations

Other powers used the monetary weapon. Germany had already counterfeited, during
the interwar period, the 100-dollar note of the Imperial Bank of Canada
(circulating in the Middle East).
[Tripod](http://currency_den.tripod.com/War_Counterfeits/war.html) Japan
conducted operations to counterfeit Chinese and Indian currency during the
Second World War. These operations confirm a constant: state counterfeiting aims
less at the total collapse of a currency (extremely difficult) than at distrust,
inflationary pressure, and the financing of clandestine operations.

## Chapter 4 — The modern era (1990 to today)

### The digital revolution and "casual counterfeiting"

From the 1990s, the democratization of **high-definition scanners, inkjet and
color laser printers, and image-editing software** shifted part of
counterfeiting into the domestic domain ("casual counterfeiting"). The shift was
spectacular: according to the US Secret Service (Department of State fact sheet,
2003), **"in 1995, less than 1% of counterfeit notes were produced on digital
printers,"** whereas in fiscal year 2001, according to the National Academies
(_Is That Real?_), **93% of dismantled counterfeiting operations used digital
processes**. This counterfeiting is generally of low quality and easily
detected, but it is massive in the number of operations.

In response, central banks developed two families of countermeasures. On the one
hand, **anti-copy devices** built into consumer hardware: the **EURion
constellation** (also called "Omron rings"), a pattern of five small circles
present on banknotes since around 1996, which photocopiers and software (Adobe
Photoshop, etc.) detect to block reproduction.
[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EURion_constellation) Identified and
named in 2002 by the Cambridge researcher **Markus Kuhn**,
[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EURion_constellation) it is
complemented by the **Counterfeit Deterrence System (CDS)**, designed by the
**Central Bank Counterfeit Deterrence Group (CBCDG)** — a group created in 2000
bringing together more than 30 central banks
[Grokipedia](https://grokipedia.com/page/EURion_constellation) — which detects a
digital watermark independently of the EURion pattern.
[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EURion_constellation) On the other
hand, increasingly sophisticated **physical security features**: 3D security
thread (the blue "MOTION" ribbon by Crane Currency on the 100-dollar bill issued
in 2013),
[Crane Currency](https://www.cranecurrency.com/news-insights/85-decline-in-us-100-counterfeits/)
color-shifting inks, holograms, micro-printing, and the move to **polymer**
(Bank of England, Canada, etc.).

### The criminal capitals of counterfeiting

**Italy (Naples / Camorra).** The Naples region — particularly the city of
**Giugliano** — is considered the European capital of the fake euro. According
to a source from the French OCRFM cited by AFP, "for several years, 90% of the
high-quality counterfeit money in circulation has come from Italy; the Camorra
has a kind of monopoly on this market."
[CoinWorld](https://www.coinworld.com/news/precious-metals/european-officials-arrest-counterfeiters-in-three-countries)
Seizures there are spectacular: in February 2018, Europol reported the seizure
in Naples of nearly 450,000 fake €50 and €100 notes, with a face value of €41
million, hidden in barrels.
[CoinWorld](https://www.coinworld.com/news/precious-metals/european-officials-arrest-counterfeiters-in-three-countries)
The €50 notes there are nicknamed "Maradonas."
[CoinWorld](https://www.coinworld.com/news/paper-money/multi-national-force-finds-millions-in-fake-euro-notes)
In 2024, a joint Carabinieri / French National Police operation supported by
Europol arrested 63 counterfeiters (the "Naples group"), linked to the Camorra.
[CoinWorld](https://www.coinworld.com/news/paper-money/multi-national-force-finds-millions-in-fake-euro-notes)

**Peru (Lima).** The US Secret Service describes Peru as the **"world's leading
manufacturer and distributor of counterfeit US currency,
[United States Secret Service](https://www.secretservice.gov/press/releases/2016/11/30-million-counterfeit-us-currency-seized-historical-secret-service)
responsible for nearly 60% of the roughly 3 billion dollars in counterfeit notes
circulating worldwide"** (statement relayed by Reuters/CNBC, 18 November 2016).
[CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2016/11/18/peru-seizes-30-million-counterfeit-dollars-in-record-bust.html)
**Operation Sunset** (15 November 2016), the largest operational seizure in the
agency's history, mobilized more than 1,500 Peruvian police officers, 54
warrants, 48 arrests, and led to the seizure of 30 million dollars in fakes and
50,000 euros, six workshops and eight presses.
[United States Secret Service](https://www.secretservice.gov/press/releases/2016/11/30-million-counterfeit-us-currency-seized-historical-secret-service)
[CoinWorld](https://www.coinworld.com/news/paper-money/secret-service-makes-its-largest-ever-counterfeit-seizure.html)
The Peruvian fake dollars, of very good quality, are sold on the black market
for $10 to $20 for a fake $100 bill,
[CGTN America](https://america.cgtn.com/2016/08/05/peru-has-become-the-new-hub-for-fake-dollars)
then exported to the United States and Latin America. The dominant technique is
**offset printing**.

**Colombia.** Long the world's "epicenter" of the fake dollar (the "golden
triangle" Bogotá–Cali–Medellín), Colombia was overtaken by Peru around 2013.
[Finance Colombia](https://www.financecolombia.com/how-counterfeit-dollars-are-overrunning-venezuela/)
In 1991, a Secret Service agent estimated that 30% of the world's fake dollars
came from Colombia.
[Christian Science Monitor](https://www.csmonitor.com/1991/0411/ocount.html) The
Colombian techniques were renowned for their quality (photographing the genuine
note, transfer onto aluminum plates, sometimes bleaching real notes reprinted in
higher denominations).
[Christian Science Monitor](https://www.csmonitor.com/1991/0411/ocount.html)
Since 1998, the Secret Service and the Colombian police have seized more than
150 million dollars in fakes.
[United States Secret Service](https://www.secretservice.gov/press/releases/2003/02/colombian-police-and-united-states-secret-service-seize-20-million)
The dollarization of Ecuador and the Venezuelan monetary chaos feed a persistent
regional demand.

**Bulgaria.** Bulgaria is a notable producer, a competitor of Italy. As early as
January 2004, Europol and North American services discovered there one of the
first workshops in the world capable of reproducing the €200 note (Varna area,
Black Sea), with production then moving to Plovdiv and Haskovo.
[Voxeurop](https://voxeurop.eu/en/content/article/1678671-glut-fake-euros)

# PART 2: STATISTICS AND GLOBAL ANALYSIS

## Worldwide overview

Measuring counterfeiting is intrinsically difficult: we know only the
**detected** fakes, never the totality of those in circulation. The authorities
therefore reason in terms of a **ratio of fakes per million genuine notes**
(PPM, "parts per million").

The general finding, counterintuitive, is that the counterfeiting of major
currencies is today **historically low** in proportion, thanks to the continuous
improvement of security features. For the euro, the ratio fell to **14 fakes per
million** notes in 2025. For the dollar, the Federal Reserve's reference study
(Ruth Judson, IFDP No. 1404, February 2025) estimates that the stock of fakes in
the United States is **at most about $30 million (1 note in 40,000)** and
**probably about $15 million (1 in 80,000)**
[federalreserve](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/ifdp/files/ifdp1404.pdf)
[Federal Reserve](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/ifdp/estimating-the-volume-of-counterfeit-us-currency-in-circulation.htm)
— a marked downward revision from the old US Treasury estimate (2006) of around
1 fake in 10,000.
[federalreserve](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/ifdp/files/ifdp1404.pdf)
[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Counterfeit_United_States_currency)
These levels are nowhere near those of the 19th-century United States.

## Eurozone: ECB data 2023–2025

The European Central Bank publishes semi-annual and annual statistics. The most
recent data show a broadly decreasing trend:

| Year | Counterfeit euro notes withdrawn | Ratio (fakes per million) | Dominant denominations          |
| ---- | -------------------------------- | ------------------------- | ------------------------------- |
| 2021 | 347,000                          | 12 (all-time low)         | €20 and €50                     |
| 2023 | 467,000                          | 16                        | €20 and €50 (>70%)              |
| 2024 | 554,000                          | 18                        | €20 and €50 (>75%)              |
| 2025 | 444,000 (–20%)                   | 14                        | €20 (27%) and €50 (53.3%) ≈ 80% |

2026).

Highlights of the 2025 data (ECB press release of 27 February 2026):

- **444,000 fakes withdrawn**, a 20% decline year-on-year, "one of the lowest
  levels ever recorded" in proportion.
  [Disruption Banking](https://www.disruptionbanking.com/2026/02/28/number-of-counterfeit-euro-banknotes-remains-low-in-2025/)
  [European Central Bank](https://www.ecb.europa.eu/press/pr/date/2026/html/ecb.pr260227~66abdc6895.fr.html)
- **14 fakes per million** genuine notes.
  [Disruption Banking](https://www.disruptionbanking.com/2026/02/28/number-of-counterfeit-euro-banknotes-remains-low-in-2025/)
  [European Central Bank](https://www.ecb.europa.eu/press/pr/date/2026/html/ecb.pr260227~66abdc6895.fr.html)
- **The €20 and €50 denominations represent about 80%** of the fakes
  [European Central Bank](https://www.ecb.europa.eu/press/pr/date/2026/html/ecb.pr260227~66abdc6895.fr.html)
  (€50: 53.3%; €20: 27%), followed by the €100 (7.9%) and the €10 (6.4%).
  [Eunews](https://www.eunews.it/en/2026/02/27/444000-counterfeit-euro-banknotes-withdrawn-from-circulation-in-2025-down-20-per-cent-from-2024/)
- Geographic distribution: **96.8%** of the fakes found in the eurozone, 2.2% in
  the rest of the EU, 1.0% elsewhere.
  [Eunews](https://www.eunews.it/en/2026/02/27/444000-counterfeit-euro-banknotes-withdrawn-from-circulation-in-2025-down-20-per-cent-from-2024/)

The 2024 increase (554,000) had been explained by the ECB as a rebound after the
exceptionally low levels of the Covid period, and not as a structural
deterioration. At the national scale, the figures are modest: in Belgium, 14,341
fakes withdrawn in 2024 (€50: 47%, €20: 34%)
[National Bank of Belgium](https://www.nbb.be/en/news-events/number-counterfeit-euro-banknotes-withdrawn-circulation-belgium-2024);
in Germany, the Bundesbank withdrew ~36,600 fakes in the first half of 2025
(value ~€2.1M),
[Deutsche Bundesbank](https://www.bundesbank.de/en/press/press-releases/more-counterfeit-money-in-circulation-962024)
about 9 counterfeit notes per 10,000 inhabitants.

## United States (dollar): Secret Service and Federal Reserve

The dollar, the world's leading currency a large share of which circulates
abroad, displays **one of the lowest counterfeiting rates** among the major
currencies. The Federal Reserve's reference study (Ruth Judson, _Estimating the
Volume of Counterfeit U.S. Currency in Circulation_, IFDP No. 1404,
February 2025) concludes:

- The **stock** of fakes in circulation in the United States is **at most about
  $30 million (1 note in 40,000)** and **probably about $15 million (1 in
  80,000)**.
  [Federal Reserve](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/ifdp/estimating-the-volume-of-counterfeit-us-currency-in-circulation.htm)
- In **fiscal year 2023**, the Secret Service recorded **about $102 million in
  counterfeit notes "passed"** on the public
  [Federal Reserve](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/ifdp/files/ifdp1404.pdf)
  (of which $102.0M domestically), about **30 cents per inhabitant** — an amount
  described as "minor" by the study.
  [Federal Reserve](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/ifdp/files/ifdp1404.pdf)
- By value, the **$100 bill** dominates the fakes passed in 2023 ($76M), against
  $14M for the $20 bill. But **the $20 bill remains the most counterfeited
  domestically** by number of pieces, while **the $100 bill is the most
  counterfeited abroad**. Nearly 90% of the fakes of $20 and lower denominations
  are of low quality.
- The stock of counterfeit $100 bills in circulation in 2023 is estimated at
  between **$2.8 and $8.5 million** (Fed processing data),
  [Federal Reserve](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/ifdp/files/ifdp1404.pdf)
  reflecting a collapse in incidence (1 in 40,000 versus far higher levels in
  the 1990s).

### The "Superdollars" / "Supernotes"

The most emblematic issue is that of the **"Supernotes"** (or _Superdollars_):
counterfeit $100 bills (and sometimes $50) of exceptional quality, **printed in
intaglio** on cotton-linen paper reproducing the red and blue fibers, the
security thread and the watermark.
[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superdollar) Detected for the first
time in 1989 (in Manila, coded C-14342 by the Secret Service),
[VICE](https://www.vice.com/en/article/north-koreas-counterfeit-benjamins-have-vanished/)
they are attributed by the US government to **North Korea** (production presumed
at the Pyongsong printing plant, supervised
[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superdollar) by "Bureau 39").

The figures must be handled with caution, as they are largely a matter of
intelligence:

- The US Secret Service estimates that North Korea may have produced **about $45
  million** in superdollars since 1989,
  [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superdollar) and that the country
  may have earned $15 to $25 million per year from them.
  [EveryCRSReport.com](https://www.everycrsreport.com/reports/RL33324.html)
- The Secret Service is said to have seized about **$50 million** in supernotes
  worldwide. [NBC News](https://www.nbcnews.com/id/wbna14232677)
- INTERPOL issued an **Orange Notice** on the Supernotes in 2006
  [INTERPOL](https://www.interpol.int/en/News-and-Events/News/2006/INTERPOL-holds-Supernote-summit-to-address-US-dollar-counterfeiting)
  and held a dedicated summit in Lyon (26 July 2006).

It must, however, be noted that there is a **controversy**: several observers
(Swiss criminal police, some experts) doubt that North Korea is the sole source,
[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superdollar) and alternative sources
(Syria, Lebanon, or presses supplied to Iran) were raised in the 1990s.
According to data compiled by Sheena Chestnut Greitens (Brookings), supernote
seizures are said to have **sharply declined, or even ceased**, since 2008,
suggesting a halt or drastic reduction of the North Korean operation.
[VICE](https://www.vice.com/en/article/north-koreas-counterfeit-benjamins-have-vanished/)
This datum should be treated as an estimate and not as an established fact.

Furthermore, non-state criminals have produced high-quality fake $100s: more
than 350,000 counterfeit $100 bills were manufactured by a British network led
by Anatasios Arnaouti, arrested in 2002.
[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superdollar)

## Asia: yuan, yen, won

Asian data are less transparent than those of the ECB or the Fed.

- **China (yuan / renminbi).** Cash remains heavily used (the People's Bank of
  China cites ~360 billion notes and coins in circulation).
  [People's Bank of China](http://www.pbc.gov.cn/en/3688110/3688172/4048338/3714906/index.html)
  Counterfeiting is a recognized problem: since 2006, the public security organs
  have, according to incomplete data, solved 4,411 cases, dismantled 44
  workshops and seized 1.54 billion yuan in fakes.
  [People's Bank of China](http://www.pbc.gov.cn/en/3688110/3688172/4048338/3714906/index.html)
  In 2017, a single seizure reportedly involved more than 214 million yuan in
  fakes.
  [Great American Coin Company®](https://www.greatamericancoincompany.com/blogs/news/the-top-5-most-counterfeited-currencies-in-the-world)
  China has integrated the EURion constellation on its notes since the 2005
  edition [Currencyguide](https://currencyguide.eu/cny-en/100cny-en.html) and
  continually strengthens the security features (color-shifting ink, intaglio).
- **Japan (yen).** Japan displays counterfeiting rates among the lowest in the
  world, attributed to highly secured notes and low monetary crime. In July
  2024, the Bank of Japan issued a new series (Series F) fitted with
  state-of-the-art 3D holograms.
  [BookMyForex](https://www.bookmyforex.com/currency-exchange/counterfeit-detection-guide/japanese-yen/)
  **Precise figures are not publicly available in a consolidated and recent
  form.**
- **South Korea (won).** **Specific data not publicly available** in
  consolidated official sources; the country has mainly been concerned, at the
  margin, as a detection zone for dollar supernotes (Seoul).

## Other regions: Latin America, Africa, Middle East

- **Latin America.** This is the most active production zone for fake dollars
  (Peru and Colombia, _cf._ Part 1). **Dollarization** (Ecuador, El Salvador,
  Panama) and monetary instability (Venezuela, hyperinflation) create a
  favorable ground for the circulation of "on-demand" fake dollars (a fake $100
  bill trading at around $10 on the Colombian-Venezuelan border).
  [Finance Colombia](https://www.financecolombia.com/how-counterfeit-dollars-are-overrunning-venezuela/)
  The network dismantled in Argentina in 2024 (see below) illustrates the
  transnational dimension.
- **Africa and the Middle East.** Consolidated official data are rare. The
  challenges there are linked to partial dollarization, the prevalence of cash,
  and cross-border circulation of fakes. "Supernotes" have historically been
  reported as circulating via the Middle East (allegations concerning
  Syria/Lebanon in the 1990s, not definitively confirmed). **An estimate to be
  treated with caution for lack of recent primary data.**

### INTERPOL and the transnational dimension

The suppression of currency counterfeiting was one of **INTERPOL's founding
mandates**, enshrined by the Geneva Convention of 1929.
[interpol](https://www.interpol.int/en/Crimes/Counterfeit-currency-and-security-documents/Counterfeit-currency)
INTERPOL assigns international index numbers to newly discovered counterfeit
notes — **except for the dollar and the euro**,
[INTERPOL](https://www.interpol.int/en/Crimes/Counterfeit-currency-and-security-documents/Counterfeit-currency)
handled respectively by the Secret Service and by the ECB/Europol. The most
significant recent operation is **Operation Jackal III** (2024): in Argentina,
the dismantling of a transnational criminal network led by Nigerians enabled the
seizure of **$1.2 million in "supernotes,"** the arrest of 72 suspects and the
freezing of about a hundred bank accounts.
[INTERPOL](https://www.interpol.int/News-and-Events/News/2024/INTERPOL-operation-strikes-major-blow-against-West-African-financial-crime)
INTERPOL's detailed annual statistics on counterfeit notes are **reserved for
authorized services** (I-24/7 network)
[INTERPOL](https://www.interpol.int/en/Crimes/Counterfeit-currency-and-security-documents/Counterfeit-currency)
and are not published as a global estimate in value. No **public quantified
global estimate from the UNODC** dedicated specifically to counterfeit money
could be identified.

## Comparison of methods: artisanal vs. industrial

| Criterion         | Artisanal counterfeiting (digital)                         | Industrial counterfeiting                                               |
| ----------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Technology        | Inkjet / laser printers, scanners, PC                      | **Offset** presses; **intaglio** for the very high end                  |
| Quality           | Low to medium; security features absent or poorly imitated | High to exceptional (supernotes)                                        |
| Unit volume       | Low (a few notes)                                          | High (millions)                                                         |
| Actors            | Isolated individuals                                       | Criminal networks (Camorra, Peruvian/Colombian gangs), states           |
| Typical locations | United States (domestic)                                   | Naples, Lima, Bogotá/Cali, Bulgaria; (supernotes: North Korea presumed) |
| Detection         | Easy (the majority of fakes)                               | Difficult, sometimes undetectable without specialized equipment         |

Intaglio — which creates the tactile relief of the ink — remains the
technological frontier that only the most advanced (or state) forgers cross.

## Economic impact: who bears the loss?

The **macroeconomic** impact of counterfeiting is today small for the major
currencies: $102 million in fakes passed in the United States in 2023 represents
less than 0.01% of the money in circulation,
[National Academies](https://www.nationalacademies.org/read/11638/chapter/5) and
the euro's 14 PPM ratio is negligible at the scale of the money supply. The
effects are mainly **microeconomic and psychological**: erosion of confidence,
occasional refusal to accept certain denominations (banks refused $100s in
Taiwan in 2004 and in Peru). [NBC News](https://www.nbcnews.com/id/wbna14232677)

The decisive question is that of the **burden of the loss**. The rule, in the
United States as in most countries, is clear: **whoever holds the fake at the
moment of its detection bears the loss**, with no reimbursement. US law requires
the surrender of counterfeit notes to the Secret Service
[Federal Reserve](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/ifdp/files/ifdp1404.pdf)
with no compensation.
[Northeastern](https://econ.sites.northeastern.edu/wiki/4785-2/measuring-inflation-and-unemployment/counterfeit-money-hidden-impacts-on-the-economy-and-inflation/)
The loss therefore cascades down: the **merchant** or the **individual** who
accepted the fake in good faith is the final loser; the banks, equipped with
detectors, withdraw and confiscate the fakes without indemnity.
[Nucoun](https://www.nucoun.com/blogs/news/what-to-do-if-merchants-receive-counterfeit-u-s-dollars)
Businesses pass these losses on to their prices.
[Serafini Law Office](https://rserafinilaw.com/the-consequences-of-counterfeiting-money-the-law-and-penalties-explained/)
Taxpayers, finally, fund the prevention costs (note redesigns, security
features, law enforcement). Counterfeiting is moreover a **vector for financing
organized crime** (drug trafficking, human trafficking, even terrorism), which
INTERPOL highlights as its most worrying dimension.
[interpol](https://www.interpol.int/en/Crimes/Counterfeit-currency-and-security-documents/Counterfeit-currency)

# Recommendations

**For individuals and merchants:**

1. Systematically apply the **"feel, look, tilt"** method recommended by the
   ECB, checking several security features (intaglio relief, watermark,
   hologram/3D thread).
2. For large cash transactions, prefer conversion into a check/transfer via a
   financial institution, or use a reliable **automatic detector** (detector
   pens are insufficient against fakes on bleached paper).
3. Focus on the highest-risk denominations: **€20 and €50** in the eurozone;
   **$20** for everyday use and **$100** for large transactions in the United
   States.

**For the authorities:**

1. Continue the **note and security renewal cycle** (polymer, 3D threads,
   machine-readable features), the only factor that has demonstrated a lasting
   effect (a decline of more than 85% in fake $100s since the 1990s
   [Crane Currency](https://www.cranecurrency.com/news-insights/85-decline-in-us-100-counterfeits/);
   the euro ratio divided by more than 4 since the 2009 peak).
2. Strengthen **transnational cooperation** (Secret Service–national police,
   Europol–Carabinieri, INTERPOL) by targeting the industrial production hubs
   (Naples, Lima).

**Alert thresholds that would change these recommendations:** a lasting rise in
the euro ratio above ~25 PPM (pre-pandemic level), the reappearance of
significant supernote seizures after their near-disappearance post-2008, or the
emergence of high-quality counterfeiting on the new series (euro "ES2," 2013
$100) would constitute signals justifying an acceleration of redesigns.

# Caveats (data limitations)

- **We measure only the detected.** All the figures (ECB, Fed, Secret Service)
  underestimate real counterfeiting by construction. The stock estimates are
  ranges, not certainties.
- **"Passed" vs. "stock" vs. "seizures."** The US $102M of 2023 are fakes
  _passed_ over one year; the _stock_ in circulation is estimated at $15–30M;
  the _seizures_ (before circulation) are yet another aggregate. Do not confuse
  them.
- **North Korean supernotes:** attribution based on intelligence, contested by
  some experts; estimates ($45M produced, $50M seized) old and uncertain.
- **Asian, African and Middle Eastern data** are patchy or unconsolidated;
  several points flagged as "data not available."
- **Historical figures** (Operation Bernhard, assignats, _free banking_) rest on
  historians' estimates and vary by source (e.g., Bernhard's production
  estimated at £132.6 to £300 million; the share of counterfeit money under
  _free banking_ estimated at a third to a half by the San Francisco Fed).
- The Secret Service's **annual seizure figures** (drawn from press reports
  citing the agency) would merit confirmation by a primary source; the Fed study
  (IFDP 1404) deliberately focuses on fakes "passed," not "seized."
